\section{The problem}
\label{sec:problem}

Expert grammaticality judgments occupy an unstable methodological category. In one description, such judgments are human responses to linguistic stimuli and therefore look like participant data. In another, such judgments are scholarly evaluations of linguistic materials and therefore look like expert ratings, editorial judgments, coding decisions, or psychometric scoring. The paper starts from that role ambiguity.

The proposed answer is not that expert judgment is exempt from scrutiny. The answer is that the scrutiny should be role-sensitive. A judge can be a participant when the research question concerns that person's reaction, processing, background, social identity, or judgment distribution. But a judge can also be an evaluator when the research question concerns the linguistic status of an item and the person's expertise is used to assess the item against disciplinary standards.

The target claim is narrow: expert judges of grammaticality should not automatically be treated as study participants merely because their judgments are human acts. The first question is what role their judgment plays in the evidential design.
