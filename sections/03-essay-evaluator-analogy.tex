\section{The essay-evaluator analogy}
\label{sec:essay-evaluator}

Educational and psychometric studies routinely distinguish the people whose performances are measured from the people who score those performances. Essay scorers, rubric raters, and adjudicators can introduce error, bias, drift, and disagreement. That does not make them participants in the same sense as the students whose essays are being evaluated.

The analogy is useful because it keeps two truths together. First, expert evaluators are part of the measurement apparatus and need to be reported as such. Second, the object of study is still the target material, not the evaluator's personal response unless the design says otherwise. In a grammaticality paper, an expert judge may function like a trained rater whose decision bears on the status of an item.

The analogy also blocks a bad inference. If raters can be unreliable, one should model, train, blind, rotate, adjudicate, or report that unreliability. One should not therefore collapse rater evaluation into participant sampling.
