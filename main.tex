\begin{abstract}
Expert judgments of grammaticality are always human acts, but they are not always participant data. When a linguist is asked to evaluate linguistic materials in virtue of professional competence, the role is closer to that of an essay scorer, translation reviewer, coding adjudicator, or psychometric rater than to that of a participant whose own behaviour, attitudes, or cognitive processing are the target of study. This paper argues for a role-sensitive distinction between participant data and expert evaluation in grammaticality research. The distinction does not remove ordinary obligations of transparency, consent, conflict management, or local ethics review where required. It instead clarifies what the evidence is evidence of: not a sample of speaker reactions, but a disciplined expert assessment of linguistic materials under stated standards.
\end{abstract}
