\begin{abstract}
A grammaticality judgment is always a human act, but it isn't always participant data. Whether the supplier is a participant or an evaluator should turn on what the judgment is evidence of: a person (reactions, processing, dialect, or response distribution) or an object (an item's status under a stated standard). There the judge is closer to an essay scorer or prescriptive annotator than to a survey respondent, and the work's systematic, reliability-checked character marks it as measurement, not a study of the judges. The United States Common Rule draws the line in its \enquote{about whom} definition of a human subject; Canada's Tri-Council Policy Statement (TCPS 2) reaches it by focus of inquiry. The proposal doesn't loosen ethics review but routes it: participant protections where persons are the object, measurement and labour standards where judgment is the instrument. It neither licenses private intuition nor erases the cases where judges are participants.
\end{abstract}
