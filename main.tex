\begin{abstract}
A grammaticality judgment is always a human act, but it isn't always participant data. Whether the person who supplies one is a research participant or an expert evaluator should turn on what the judgment is evidence of: a person (their reactions, processing, dialect, or the distribution of their responses) or an object (the linguistic status of an item, assessed against a stated standard). In the second case the judge is closer to an essay scorer, diagnostic rater, or prescriptive annotator than to a survey respondent, and the systematic, multi-rater, reliability-checked character of such work marks it as measurement rather than a study of the judges. United States policy encodes one version of the line in its \enquote{about whom} definition of a human subject; Canadian policy draws a parallel line by focus of inquiry. The proposal doesn't loosen ethics review but routes it: participant protections where persons are the object of study, and qualification, calibration, independence, and labour protections where judgment is part of the measurement procedure. It neither licenses private intuition nor erases the cases in which judges are participants.
\end{abstract}
