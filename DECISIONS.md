# DECISIONS

2026-06-18 — Created the project as a standalone paper under `papers/Expert_Grammaticality_Judges_Are_Evaluators_Not_Participants/`. Reason: the argument is a general methodological and ethics-facing claim about the evidential role of expert grammaticality judges, not a subargument of one grammaticality paper.

2026-06-18 — Working title set to *Expert Grammaticality Judges Are Evaluators, Not Participants: Grammaticality Judgments, Rater Roles, and Human-Subjects Review*. Reason: the title foregrounds the participant/evaluator distinction while leaving room for ethics-review and measurement-quality implications.

2026-06-18 — Central framing fixed as role-based, not status-based. Reason: expert judges are participants when the study targets their responses, processing, background, or distribution; they are evaluators when their expertise is used to assess linguistic materials.

2026-06-18 — Added the essay-evaluator / psychometric-rater analogy as the guiding comparison. Reason: scorer reliability, bias, drift, and adjudication are real methodological issues, but they do not make scorers participants in the same sense as the people whose performances are being measured.

2026-06-18 — Set project to central bibliography and central house style by symlink. Reason: current paper infrastructure avoids local house-style snapshots and uses `references-local.bib` only for verified project-specific entries.

2026-06-18 — Gathered and verified the core source set against primary sources; six entries added to `references-local.bib` (Röttger et al. 2022; Kaushik, Lipton & London 2023; McCaffrey et al. 2022 ETS; AERA/APA/NCME 2014; TCPS 2 2022; US Common Rule). Verbatim extracts in `notes/source-verification.md`. Schütze 2016 and Sprouse et al. 2013 already central.

2026-06-18 — Argument finding (source-driven): the systematicity-inversion line is supported from inside TCPS 2 itself. The Chapter 2 Application to Article 2.5 states that "choice of methodology and/or intent or ability to publish findings are not factors that determine whether an activity is research requiring ethics review." So formality/multi-rater/statistics do not by themselves make judgment collection participant research. Use this rather than resting only on the essay-scorer analogy.

2026-06-18 — Correction to a GPT-5.5 claim: the "Language Consulting is Standard Professional Practice" exemption document does NOT draw its line at formal surveys/questionnaires/statistics. Its actual limits are vulnerable populations / power differentials and personal-information topics. The disciplinary precedent is therefore friendlier to the strong claim than the discussion suggested. Provenance of that document (Western NMREB 3-G-006 vs a Linguistics-department guideline built on *Giving Voice to the Spectrum*) is unresolved and must be confirmed before citation.

2026-06-19 — Added six arguments after a gap-scan. (1) Labor axis, §7: classifying a judge as an evaluator settles human-subjects-*data* status, not labour/personnel status; a pressed grad student is no one's subject but is still owed anti-coercion protection, on a separate axis (`kaushik2023crowdworkers`). (2) Reliability-is-about-the-raters rebuttal, §3: inter-rater agreement characterises the instrument to license inference about the object, as a rater facet does in psychometrics. (3) No-standard caveat, §4: the evaluator role presupposes a statable, challengeable criterion; absent one it collapses into the private authority the paper disavows (`dabrowska2010`, naive v. expert intuitions). (4) Peer-reviewer parallel, §3: a referee's embedded acceptability judgment never makes them a participant. (5) Wired in the descriptive/prescriptive annotation mapping, §2 (`rottger2022annotation`): descriptive ≈ participant, prescriptive ≈ evaluator. (6) Projectibility, §4 (`Goodman1955`): the role fixes what a judgment projects to (item status vs population distribution), so it governs licit inference, not just ethics. All Crossref/primary-verified; build clean at 8 pages.

2026-06-19 — Added a reductio to §5: if any judgment relevant to the research question makes the judge a participant, the sole author of a theoretical syntax paper is a participant in their own study (self-consent, right to withdraw, REB review of self), which is absurd and which no board imposes. Reinforced with a parity pair (author judges 50 sentences vs colleague judges the same 50: identical evidential role, only the producer differs) to show the trigger is focus of inquiry, not the human act. Guardrailed against overreach: the reductio defeats "judgment → participant," not "lone intuition is good evidence"; the reliability worry is the measurement point of §1, met by calibration/replication, not by recasting the author as a subject.

2026-06-18 — Reframed §6 after reviewer critique. The earlier draft used Article 2.5 and the "methodology is not the test" gloss to defend the participant/evaluator line, but those settle *research vs non-research*, not *participant vs evaluator within research*. §6 now runs the from/about logic through the Canadian wording directly, via the verified PRE "Scope" interpretation (`pre_scope`): "the information is the focus of the research, not the views of the staff member." The methodology gloss is demoted to a secondary point (it only blocks systematicity→participant). The PRE boundary ("personal opinions outside the scope of their job roles" → REB) is named as the pressure point the argument must respect. Reason: closes the most likely reviewer objection (~0.7 confidence it would be flagged).

2026-06-18 — Named the interlocutor in §1 (Gibson & Fedorenko's experimental-syntax critique: informal intuition is a weak base, sample naive speakers), engaged Rapoport-style: the reliability worry is real but is a measurement concern (calibration), not a reason to reclassify the judge as the object of study. Verified entries added (`gibsonfedorenko2010`, `gibsonfedorenko2013`, `gibson2011mturk`); reliability reply via central `sprouse2013`/`schutze2016`. Reason: the paper engaged no linguistics interlocutor; an editor and linguistics reviewer would expect one (~0.85).

2026-06-18 — Jurisdictional asymmetry confirmed as a structural point: US Common Rule defines a human subject as one "about whom" data are obtained (45 CFR 46.102(e)(1)); TCPS 2's participant definition has no "about" qualifier. The from/about distinction is explicit in US law and absent from the Canadian text, which (given Humber's TCPS 2 jurisdiction) the paper must address head-on.
